Introduction

The vanilla fastICA assumes a noiseless model, or at best a model with fixed noise level \(\sigma^2=1\). In this analysis, we investigate what the smallest signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) above which fastICA or EBICA can reliably recover the true sources.

Related works

Relevant references include Li and Wei (2022), Li et al. (2023) and Celentano et al. (2023). These papers study AMP and related algorithms for spiked Wigner models. A canonical model in Li and Wei (2022) is

\[ M = \lambda vv^\top + W, \quad W_{ij}\sim N(0, 1/n), \quad W_{ii}\sim N(0, 2/n), \quad v_{i}\sim \text{Unif}(\pm 1/\sqrt{n}). \] Here \(\lambda\) is the SNR parameter. The phase transition is at \(\lambda=1\): below this threshold, the Bayes-optimal estimate has asymptotically zero correlation with the spike, while above it nontrivial correlation is achievable. The finite-sample AMP result in Li and Wei (2022) assumes a slightly stronger condition \(\lambda > 1 + \log(n)n^{-1/16}\), but this extra gap is a proof condition rather than the sharp asymptotic threshold.

The simulation here starts from a noisy rank-\(K\) model,

\[ Y = LF^\top + E, \quad E_{ij}\sim N(0,\sigma^2), \] with \(L_{ik}\in\{\pm 1\}\) and \(F_{jk}\sim N(0,c)\). Conditional on \(L\),

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[\frac1p YY^\top \;\middle|\; L\right]=\sigma^2 I_n+c LL^\top. \]

Thus the model induces a spiked covariance. The Li–Wei threshold does not directly apply to our simulation model, but Appendix A gives a heuristic reduction from the off-diagonal Gram matrix to a spiked Wigner model. Under this reduction, the effective Wigner spike strength is approximately

\[ \lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx \frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{signal}}\sqrt{p/n}} {\sqrt{\sigma^4+2\bar c\sigma^2}}, \] where \(\lambda_{\mathrm{signal}}\) is a signal eigenvalue of \((LF^\top)(LF^\top)^\top/p\) and

\[ \bar c = \frac1n \operatorname{tr}\left((LF^\top)(LF^\top)^\top/p\right) \]

is the average signal variance per row. For recovering any one of the true sources, a conservative scale is given by the weakest nonzero signal eigenvalue, although the rectangular covariance model has its own BBP scaling and this mapping should be viewed as a heuristic connection.

With the phase-transition threshold \(\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}=1\), the corresponding phase-transition noise variance is approximately

\[ \sigma^2_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx \sqrt{\bar c^2+\lambda_{\mathrm{signal}}^2p/n} -\bar c. \]

ebcd_rank_one_random_init <- function(X, init_Z = NULL, 
                                      tau = NULL, est_precision = TRUE,
                                      tol_backfit = 1e-6,
                                      maxiter_backfit = 100) {
  ebcd_obj <- ebcd_random_init(X = X, Z = init_Z, tau = tau, ebnm_fn = ebnm_rademacher) |>
    ebcd_backfit(
      tol = tol_backfit,
      maxiter = maxiter_backfit,
      est_precision = est_precision
    )
  return(ebcd_obj)
}

run_ebcd_noise_experiment <- function(noise_var, noise_id) {
  set.seed(1000 + noise_id)
  X_noise <- signal + rnorm(n * p, mean = 0, sd = sqrt(noise_var))
  X1_noise <- preprocess(X_noise, n.comp = K)
  X1_noise <- rbind(X1_noise, rep(1, ncol(X1_noise)))

  max_abs_cor <- numeric(n_starts)

  for (start_id in 1:n_starts) {
    set.seed(start_id)
    init_Z <- rnorm(nrow(X1_noise))
    ebcd.fit <- ebcd_rank_one_random_init(
      X1_noise,
      init_Z = init_Z,
      tau = 1,
      est_precision = FALSE,
      maxiter_backfit = 100
    )
    max_abs_cor[start_id] <- max(abs(cor(ebcd.fit$EL, L)))
  }

  data.frame(
    noise_variance = noise_var,
    n_success = sum(max_abs_cor > 0.9),
    n_starts = n_starts,
    success_rate = mean(max_abs_cor > 0.9),
    mean_max_abs_cor = mean(max_abs_cor),
    max_max_abs_cor = max(max_abs_cor)
  )
}

ebcd_noise_results <- do.call(

Appendix A: Heuristic Wigner reduction

This derivation is heuristic. It maps the off-diagonal part of the rectangular Gram matrix to the spiked Wigner scaling in Li and Wei (2022).

Write

\[ S=LF^\top, \quad Y=S+E. \]

Then

\[ \frac1pYY^\top = \frac1p SS^\top + \frac1p S E^\top + \frac1p E S^\top + \frac1p EE^\top. \]

Let \(\theta\) be an eigenvalue of \(SS^\top/p\) with unit eigenvector \(q\). The corresponding signal term is

\[ \theta qq^\top. \]

For \(i\neq j\), the three off-diagonal noise terms are

\[ \frac1p\sum_{\ell=1}^p S_{i\ell}E_{j\ell}, \quad \frac1p\sum_{\ell=1}^p E_{i\ell}S_{j\ell}, \quad \frac1p\sum_{\ell=1}^p E_{i\ell}E_{j\ell}. \]

They have mean zero. Conditional on \(S\), their variances are

\[ \frac{\sigma^2\|S_{i\cdot}\|^2}{p^2}, \quad \frac{\sigma^2\|S_{j\cdot}\|^2}{p^2}, \quad \frac{\sigma^4}{p}. \]

Using the average row energy

\[ \bar c = \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\|S_{i\cdot}\|^2}{p} = \frac1n \operatorname{tr}(SS^\top/p), \]

the typical off-diagonal Gram noise variance is approximately

\[ \frac{\sigma^4+2\bar c\sigma^2}{p}. \]

Multiplying the centered off-diagonal Gram matrix by

\[ \alpha \approx \sqrt{\frac{p}{n(\sigma^4+2\bar c\sigma^2)}} \]

puts the noise variance on the Wigner scale \(1/n\). Under the same rescaling, the signal becomes approximately \(\alpha\theta qq^\top\), so

\[ \lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx \alpha\theta \approx \frac{\theta\sqrt{p/n}}{\sqrt{\sigma^4+2\bar c\sigma^2}}. \]

Setting \(\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx 1\) with \(\theta=\lambda_{\mathrm{signal}}\) gives

\[ \sigma^4 + 2\bar c\sigma^2 \approx \lambda_{\mathrm{signal}}^2 p/n, \]

and therefore

\[ \sigma^2_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx \sqrt{\bar c^2+\lambda_{\mathrm{signal}}^2p/n} -\bar c. \]

In the rank-one special case, \(\bar c\approx c\) and \(\lambda_{\mathrm{signal}}\approx cn\), so this reduces to

\[ \sigma^2_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx c\left(\sqrt{1+np}-1\right). \]
